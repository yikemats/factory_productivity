Factory Geek Journalist's Aerial Comparison:
Tesla vs. Toyota's Latest Factories


By Yuka Ikematsu | @YukaI_NYC
August 8, 2023

Have you heard of Toyota Motor Corporation? Of course, you do. Established in 1937 in Koromo (now Toyota City),Japan, Toyota grew to become the world's largest automotive manufacturer. It stands as one of the most renowned Japanese companies worldwide.

Achieving a production volume of 10.48 million vehicles in 2022, Toyota secured its position as the world's top producer for the third consecutive year. Comparatively, in the same year, American electric vehicle manufacturer Tesla, founded in 2003, produced 1.37 million vehicles, highlighting a substantial difference.

The reason behind Toyota's ascent to becoming the world's largest automaker lies in the efficient production system it fostered internally since the 1960s, known as the "Toyota Production System." Toyota demonstrated to the world that efficient production methods are an absolute necessity for a company to become a global leader in manufacturing.

As a Japanese business journalist, I have extensively covered Toyota's improvement methodology, known as "Kaizen." My passion for factories started from childhood, playing inside my father's small factory in Chiba, Japan. Even though the factory was located a 30-minute drive away from my actual home, it was part of my dear home. After becoming a journalist, I actively engaged with the people on the factory floor, collaborating with them on Kaizen practices as part of my interview process (sometimes, it was more than the interview!).

Okay, I admit. I am a "factory geek."


A generational shift happening?


Toyota's remarkable growth seemed untouchable by other companies, but it is now closely pursued by Tesla "in a way". If you look at its stock price, it has comfortably surpassed Toyota's.

As of August 4, 2023, Tesla's stock price on the New York Stock Exchange stands at $170.79, while Toyota's stock price lags at $253.86, making Tesla's 1.5 times higher than Toyota's.

What captivates investors about Tesla? The key lies in Tesla's rapid development of its production system, surpassing even the renowned Toyota Production System. The effect of this innovation is evident in Tesla's performance, as its profit margin in 2022 reached 25.6%, while Toyota's remained at 17.0%.

I wrote an article to comprehend the remarkable nature of Tesla's production system before. So I want to examine the differences between Toyota and Tesla's factories from a "bird's eye".

Fortunately, both companies completed the construction of factories in the United States about the same time.

Manufacturing

Tesla Giga Texas

Let's find out the locations.

In April 2022, Tesla started the operation of its massive "Giga Texas" factory in Austin, Texas. It now produces the SUV "Model Y" and the pickup truck "Cybertruck."

In September 2021, Toyota, in collaboration with Mazda, commenced production at their factory in Huntsville, Alabama. This facility manufactures Toyota's "Corolla Cross" and Mazda's "CX-50."

The two GIFs above are set to the same size, from which another interesting fact can be gleaned. Tesla is composed of one large factory building, while Toyota is composed of several factory buildings.

In fact, Toyota's factory is divided into separate buildings for different manufacturing processes carried out by its suppliers.

Even the white lines that connect these structures are visible. These lines represent roads that connect to the assembly line.


Based on the findings above, one can say that while Tesla has largely internalized the automotive production process, Toyota relies on external suppliers for component manufacturing.

While this is a personal perspective from a factory enthusiast, internalizing the manufacturing process as much as possible is advantageous. This approach reduces the effort required to transport goods and convey specific requirements for desired items to external parties, leading to more efficient production. Tesla is significantly advantaged in this regard.

Even Toyota, which has reigned as the champion, cannot afford to be complacent. The day for a generational shift in production volume might not be as distant as one may think.
